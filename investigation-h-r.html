The H-R diagram -
a window to the stars

LSST Teacher Guide

Description and Learning Objectives

This investigation evolves the discovery that stars have fundamentally different properties (size, energy output, color and temperature). It introduces an important tool, the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) Diagram. Students will use LSST data to create an H-R Diagram, then use it to explore patterns in star characteristics. They will develop a set of rules that apply to stars on different parts of the H-R Diagram.

Students will not have an appreciation for why the H-R Diagram is such a valuable tool unless they use it in the same way as astronomers, to find information about star distances, relative ages and compositions. We recommend that you consider at least one of the follow-up investigations listed below to underscore its importance in astronomy.

  • Launch Investigation

Related LSST Investigations

  • Sizing up the Sun (extension)
  • Distance and Age of a Star Cluster (extension)

Level

Introductory (Level 1)

Time

1-2 hours or class periods

Learning Outcomes

  • There are a larger number of cooler (red) stars compared to hotter (blue) stars.
  • Considering stars of similar temperatures, those above the Main Sequence have larger sizes, while those below are smaller. This can explain the differences in luminosity.

NGSS Disciplinary Core Ideas

HS-ESS1-1A: Stars go through stages which can be described by changes in their properties. Examples of evidence include observations of the masses and lifetimes of other stars. The study of stars’ light spectra and brightness is used to identify compositional elements of stars, their movements, and their distances from Earth.

Prerequisite Concepts

  • The relationship between color & temperature in stars (Wien's Law, blackbody curves)
  • The relationship between energy output and temperature in stars (Stefan- Boltzman Law)
  • The relationship between the energy output of a star and its size (Luminosity equation)
  • Students should have previously been introduced to the H-R Diagram, if only as a graphic with labeled areas (Main Sequence, Giants, White Dwarfs). Alternatively, they could be provided one as reference while doing this investigation.

Instructional Videos

Introduction: Stars (1 minute) depicts images of stars and clusters, and asks the questions, "How are stars different from each other, and what may cause these differences?"

Assessment: They Might Be Giants (3 minutes without pauses) shows a group of students discussing what they learned about stars and the H-R Diagram. Your students are periodically asked to vote on who has the correct idea, and explain why.

Teacher Notes

Some cluster data sets may not reveal the classic distribution of stars shown on an H-R Diagram. This may be due to a very young cluster (perhaps few giants or white dwarfs). By comparing the H-R Diagrams of the stars clusters generated by a number of other class members, the patterns will become evident.

Common Student Misconceptions

  • The H-R Diagram is not an actual representation of the physical arrangement of stars in space.
  • Each point on the diagram represents one star.

Related Concepts

These concepts relate to this investigation either as prerequisite knowledge, as extensions, or as future concepts to be addressed. They are provided here as a possible tool for use in instructional sequencing.

Blackbody curves, Wien’s law, Stefan-Boltzmann law, apparent vs. absolute magnitude, spectral classes of stars, star clusters, characteristics of the Sun, distances to stars, stellar evolution, variable stars and supernovae, Population I and II stars.

Common Student Questions

  • Why are there so many more red stars (or so few blue stars)?
  • Why are not all white dwarf stars white? What does it mean if a white dwarf is not white?
  • Why aren’t there any purple stars?
  • Why do we see white stars instead of green?
  • Why is it that the stars form groups and are not randomly scattered over the diagram?
  • Why are most of the stars on the Main Sequence?
  • Why is the temperature axis on the H-R Diagram backwards (bigger numbers near the origin)?

Background & Resources

The H-R Diagram is a plot of the temperature of a star (on the x- axis) vs. its energy output (on the y-axis). In this investigation, stellar energy is listed in luminosity values. There are many variations of this plot, with additional or alternate descriptions along both axes.

Most of the stars on an H-R Diagram occupy a region from upper right to lower left, the Main Sequence. The stars above the Main Sequence (the giants and supergiants) are brighter than Main Sequence stars because of their larger size, while the stars below it (the white dwarfs) are dimmer due to their very small size.

The groups of stars on the H-R Diagram represent different stages of stellar evolution. This investigation provides a launch point to learn about how stars evolve, and may be revisited once the topic has been introduced.

Extensions

  • Sizing up the Sun compares the Sun to other stars on an H-R Diagram and develops an understanding of how the H-R Diagram may be used to determine the life span, mass, size and density of other stars.
  • Distance and Age of a Star Cluster uses the same basic process as astronomers to determine distance to a star cluster and its approximate age.

Ideas for student research projects originating from this investigation or its extensions:

  • Looking at the spatial distribution of clusters vs. their size, age or distance
  • Comparing globular to open clusters – their relative age, size, distance, or appearance
  • Studying a specific sub-population of the H-R Diagram with different clusters. Examples could include planetary nebulae, AGB stars, blue stragglers, Cepheids, Young Stellar Objects, etc.
  • Constructing and comparing H-R Diagrams of extended clusters, dwarf galaxies or star cluster complexes
  • Studying star clusters in the Large Magellanic Cloud or Small Magellanic Cloud
