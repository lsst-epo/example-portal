The H-R diagram -
a window to the stars

Teacher's Guide

Description and Learning Objectives

This activity evolves the discovery that stars have fundamentally different properties (size, energy output, color and temperature). It introduces an important tool, the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) Diagram. Students will use LSST data to create an H-R Diagram, then use it to explore patterns in star characteristics. They will develop a set of rules that apply to stars on different parts of the H-R Diagram.

Students will not have an appreciation for why the H-R Diagram is such a valuable tool unless they use it in the same way as astronomers, to find information about star distances, relative ages and compositions. We recommend that you consider at least one of the follow-up activities listed below to underscore its importance in astronomy.

Related LSST Investigations

  • Sizing up the Sun (Introductory)
  • Ages of Star Clusters (Intermediate)
  • Finding the Distance to a Star Cluster (Intermediate)

Level

Introductory

Time

1-2 hours or class periods

Learning Outcomes

  • There is a relationship between the color of a star and its temperature.
  • A star's luminosity depends on its temperature and size.
  • There is a larger number of cooler (red) stars compared to hotter (blue) stars.
  • Most stars group along a diagonal strip running from the upper left to the lower right of the diagram. This is called the Main Sequence
  • Considering stars of similar temperatures, those above the Main Sequence have larger sizes, while those below are smaller. This can explain the differences in luminosity.

Prerequisite Concepts

  • The relationship between color & temperature in stars (Wien's Law, blackbody curves)
  • The relationship between energy output and temperature in stars (Stefan- Boltzman Law)
  • The relationship between the energy output of a star and its size (Luminosity equation)

Instructional Videos

Introduction: Stars depicts images of stars and clusters, and asks the questions, “How are stars different from each other, and what may cause these differences?"

Assessment: They Might Be Giants shows a group of students discussing what they learned about stars and the H-R Diagram. Your students are then asked to vote on who has the correct idea, and why.

Related Concepts

Blackbody curves, Wien’s law, Stefan- Boltzmann law, apparent vs. absolute magnitude, spectral classes of stars, star clusters, characteristics of the Sun, distances to stars, stellar evolution, variable stars and supernovae.

Teacher Notes

The individual student data sets may be too small of a sample to reveal the classic distribution of stars shown on an H-R Diagram, but when the larger LSST data set of stars are imported or combined from class data, the patterns will become evident.

Common Student Misconceptions

  • The H-R Diagram is not an actual representation of the physical arrangement of stars in space.
  • Each point on the diagram represents one star.

Common Student Questions

  • Why are there so many more red stars (or so few blue stars)?
  • Why are there more than one group of red (or white) stars?
  • Why are not all white dwarf stars white? What does it mean if a white dwarf is not white?
  • Why aren’t there any purple stars?
  • Why do we see white stars instead of green?
  • Why is it that the stars form groups and are not randomly scattered over the diagram?
  • What do the groups represent?
  • Why are most of the stars on the Main Sequence?
  • Why is the temperature axis on the H-R Diagram backwards (bigger numbers near the origin)?

Options for Differentiation

This activity may be differentiated by assigning the difficulty level of the data: (easy, average or complex).

Background & Resources

The H-R Diagram is a plot of the temperature of a star (on the x- axis) vs. its energy output (on the y-axis). In this investigation, stellar energy is listed in luminosity values. Luminosity is a ratio of the star’s energy output to the Sun. A star with a luminosity of 100 is 100 times brighter than the Sun. Luminosity depends on two factors: the area and temperature of a star.

Most of the stars on an H-R Diagram occupy a region from upper right to lower left, the Main Sequence. The stars above the Main Sequence (the giants and supergiants) are brighter than Main Sequence stars because of their larger size, while the stars below it (the white dwarfs) are dimmer due to their very small size.

The groups of stars on the H-R Diagram represent different stages of stellar evolution. This activity provides a launch point to learn about how stars evolve, and may be revisited once the topic has been introduced.

Extensions

  • Sizing up the Sun compares the Sun to other stars on an H-R Diagram and develops an understanding of how the H-R Diagram may be used to determine the life span, mass, size and density of other stars.
  • Ages of Star Clusters uses the same process as astronomers to determine the approximate age of stars in a cluster. Comparisons of two or more clusters reveal which are more evolved.
  • Finding the Distance to a Star Cluster uses the same process as astronomers to determine distance to a star cluster.
  • Other ideas: planetary nebulae, AGB stars, blue stragglers, Cepheids, YSOs, extended clusters, ultra compact dwarf galaxies (UCG), star cluster complexes, looking at the spatial distribution of clusters vs. their age or metallicity, studying star clusters in the LMC or SMC comparing globular to open clusters – age and metallicity

Keywords

Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) Diagram, stars, luminosity, Main Sequence, white dwarfs, supergiants

Core Ideas

The study of stars’ light spectra and brightness is used to identify compositional elements of stars, their movements, and their distances from Earth.
